# Sample 3 — book notes: Antifragile (Taleb)

Re-read in 2026 after first read in 2014. Older self vs younger self
finds different things to fight with.

## Core claim

Systems sit on a spectrum: fragile (gets worse with stress), robust
(unchanged), or antifragile (gets stronger). Most engineering culture
optimizes for robustness; antifragility is the more interesting goal
because it converts variance into compounding advantage.

## Sharper now than at first read

- The "via negativa" principle (remove harmful things, don't add
  beneficial things) is a much stronger framing for system design
  than I appreciated in 2014. It's particularly relevant for incident
  response: most incidents are made worse by additive interventions
  (more logging, more retries, more alerts) when the root cause is
  removable.
- The barbell strategy — extreme caution on most positions, extreme
  risk on a small minority — maps onto product strategy nicely.
  Robust core + experimental edges; never invest in the murky middle.

## Less convinced now

- The author's confidence about which fields "lack skin in the game"
  is uneven. Some critiques land; others sound like axe-grinding.
- The "Lindy effect" reasoning (old things have proved their
  durability; new things haven't) is a useful heuristic but treated as
  an axiom. It's not — there are reverse-Lindy domains where the new
  thing is genuinely better.
